What to Expect

Practical guidance for this time. Every family's experience is different — take what feels helpful and leave the rest.

  • Create a comfortable, quiet space for your loved one with familiar blankets and photographs nearby
  • Keep a small table by the bedside for water, medications, and personal items
  • Set up a comfortable chair or recliner for the person keeping watch overnight
  • Consider soft lighting — a warm lamp rather than overhead fluorescents
  • Have extra linens, towels, and pillows easily accessible
  • It is okay to limit visits when the family needs rest — share visiting hours on this page
  • Ask one person to be the "door greeter" so the primary caregiver does not have to manage arrivals
  • Visitors often want to help but do not know how — point them to the task board above
  • Short visits (15–30 minutes) are often easier for everyone than long ones
  • It is perfectly fine to post an update saying "today is a quiet day" when visits are not welcome
  • You cannot pour from an empty cup — accepting help is not weakness, it is wisdom
  • Try to eat regular meals, even small ones. Let people bring you food.
  • Step outside for fresh air, even for five minutes. The world will not fall apart.
  • Sleep when you can. Ask someone to take over for a few hours — that is what the visiting schedule is for.
  • It is normal to feel exhausted, frustrated, or guilty. All of those feelings belong here.
  • Changes in breathing, appetite, and wakefulness are natural parts of this process
  • Your hospice team or palliative care nurse is there for questions at any hour — do not hesitate to call
  • Being present is enough. You do not need to say the perfect thing.
  • When the time comes, Neshama can help you transition to memorial and shiva coordination — your contacts and meal calendar will carry over
