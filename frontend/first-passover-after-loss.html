The First Pesach After Losing Someone

A gentle guide for navigating the holidays while grieving

The Empty Chair at the Seder Table

The seder is built around togetherness. Every part of it — the reading, the singing, the questions, the meal — is meant to be shared. And when someone is missing from that table, every ritual reminds you.

The four questions they always asked. The song only they knew the words to. The charoset recipe in their handwriting, still taped inside a kitchen cupboard. The chair where they always sat.

If this is your first Pesach without them, you already know what this feels like. You don't need anyone to explain the weight of it. What you might need is permission to feel it — and to know you're not alone in it.

What Jewish Tradition Says

Jewish law actually holds space for this. During shloshim — the first thirty days of mourning — mourners are exempted from certain holiday obligations. You're not expected to celebrate as usual. The tradition understands that joy and grief can sit side by side, and it doesn't ask you to pretend otherwise.

If your loss is recent, speak with your rabbi about what applies to your situation. There are halachic guidelines for mourning during Yom Tov, and they exist precisely because our tradition knows that holidays can be the hardest days.

Even beyond the formal mourning period, Judaism recognises that grief doesn't follow a calendar. The holidays come whether you're ready or not.

What You Might Feel

You might not want to host this year. You might not want to go anywhere, either. You might dread the seder, or you might want it desperately — and feel guilty about both.

You might cry during Dayeinu. You might feel a wave of anger when everyone else is singing. You might sit at the table and feel nothing at all, and wonder what's wrong with you. Nothing is wrong with you.

Grief during the holidays is disorienting because everyone around you is celebrating, and you're carrying something they can't see. You might feel pressure to be "okay" for the family, to keep things normal for the children, to hold it together during the seder. You don't have to.

There is no wrong way to do this. Whatever you're feeling is the right thing to be feeling.

Ways to Honour Their Memory at the Seder

Some families find comfort in creating a small space for the person who's missing. Others prefer not to — and that's equally valid. Here are some things families have done:

  • Set a place for them — an empty chair with a place setting, or a photograph beside a candle. Some families find this comforting; others find it too painful. Trust what feels right for yours.
  • Light a yahrzeit candle before the holiday candles — a quiet moment of remembrance before the celebration begins.
  • Tell their favourite Pesach story — the haggadah asks us to tell stories. Tell one of theirs.
  • Keep one of their traditions alive — their recipe, their melody for a particular song, the way they hid the afikoman, the seat they always chose.
  • Say their name — during the seder, during the meal, whenever it feels right. Saying their name keeps them present.
A thought: You don't have to do any of these things. And you don't have to decide in advance. Some families plan a tribute and find it too hard in the moment. Others find themselves spontaneously sharing a memory mid-meal. Both are fine. Let the evening unfold.

What the Community Can Do

If someone you know is facing their first Pesach after a loss, here are ways to be present for them:

  • Invite, don't pressure — extend the invitation warmly and make it clear there's no obligation. "We'd love to have you, and we completely understand if you'd rather not" gives them room to decide.
  • Offer to bring a dish — if they're hosting, the logistics of Pesach preparation on top of grief can be overwhelming. Offer something specific: "Can I bring the soup?" is more helpful than "Let me know if you need anything."
  • Mention the person who's missing — the family wants to hear their name. Don't avoid it because you're afraid of making them sad. They're already sad. Hearing you remember is a comfort.
  • Check in the day after the seder — everyone rallies for the holiday itself. The day after can be the hardest, when the house is quiet and the absence is louder.
  • Don't say "at least they're not suffering" — even if it's true, it's not helpful in the moment. "I'm thinking of you" or "I miss them too" says more.

You Don't Have to Be Okay

Grief doesn't pause for holidays. It doesn't wait until the seder is over. It doesn't care about your seating chart or your brisket or whether the kids found the afikoman.

Give yourself permission to step away from the table. To skip a song. To sit quietly while everyone else is laughing. To leave early, or to stay late because going home to an empty house feels worse.

The haggadah says b'chol dor v'dor — in every generation. Your generation includes this one. The one where the seder has an empty chair. The one where the songs sound different because their voice is missing.

This Pesach will be hard. It won't always be this hard. But right now, it is — and that's okay.
