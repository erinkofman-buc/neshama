# Funeral Home Outreach Playbook
**Neshama — Partner Outreach Strategy**
Updated: March 25, 2026

This playbook is for Jordana and Erin. It covers timing, psychology, scripting, objection handling, and the sensitive scraping conversation. Based on deep research into funeral industry culture, deathcare technology partnerships, Canadian obituary law, and funeral director psychology.

---

## 1. Timing Recommendation

### When to Call After the Email

The emails went out March 24. Here is the recommended timeline:

- **Do NOT call before Thursday March 27.** Three business days minimum. Calling sooner feels like pressure, and funeral directors are already skeptical of vendors who don't respect their time.
- **Ideal first call window: Thursday March 27 or Friday March 28.** This gives them time to have seen the email (or not) without feeling ambushed.
- **If no answer on the call, do NOT leave a voicemail the first time.** Try again the next business day. If no answer on the second attempt, leave a brief voicemail.
- **If no response to email or calls by Day 10 (April 3), send one short follow-up email.** After that, wait. These are long-term relationships, not sales cycles.

### Best Time of Day to Call

- **Tuesday through Thursday, 10:00 AM to 11:30 AM.** This is after the morning rush (families calling to report overnight deaths, staff coordinating same-day services) but before lunch and afternoon visitations/funerals.
- **Avoid Mondays.** Funeral homes are slammed on Mondays — weekend deaths stack up, families come in for arrangements, staff is stretched thin.
- **Avoid Fridays after noon.** Jewish funeral homes may be winding down for Shabbat. Steeles and Benjamin's will be preparing for the weekend.
- **Never call during a funeral or visitation.** If you hear background noise that sounds like a service, say "It sounds like you might be busy — is there a better time for me to call back?" and hang up gracefully.

### Day of Week Ranking (best to worst)

1. Tuesday mid-morning
2. Wednesday mid-morning
3. Thursday mid-morning
4. Friday before 11 AM (Jewish funeral homes only — be mindful of Shabbat prep)
5. Monday (last resort)

## 2. What Funeral Directors Actually Care About

This is the most important section. Everything Jordana says on the phone must connect to one of these core values.

### Their Core Identity

Funeral directors see themselves as **community servants, not businesspeople**. They chose this profession because they want to help families during the worst moments of their lives. The psychological profile of funeral directors is characterized by deep empathy, resilience, conscientiousness, and emotional regulation. They view their work as a calling, not a job.

### What They Care About Most

1. **The families they serve.** Everything comes back to this. If you can show that Neshama helps families, you have their attention.

2. **Their reputation in the community.** Jewish funeral homes in Toronto and Montreal serve a tight community. Word travels. They care deeply about how they are perceived by rabbis, synagogue boards, and community leaders.

3. **Control over how the deceased are presented.** Obituaries are sacred documents. The family trusted the funeral home with this content. Any suggestion that someone else is handling it without their knowledge will trigger a protective response.

4. **Not being exploited.** The industry has been burned — badly — by obituary piracy (Echovita, Afterlife Network). In 2019, a Canadian court awarded $20 million in damages against Afterlife Network for scraping and monetizing obituaries. The FSAC issued a formal advisory in February 2025 about protecting obituaries in the digital age. Canadian funeral directors are on high alert about this issue.

5. **Simplicity.** They are already overwhelmed. They do not want another platform, another login, another dashboard, another thing to manage. If Neshama requires nothing from them, that is a feature, not a limitation.

6. **Being respected as professionals.** Funeral directors report that the general public holds stigmas and negative stereotypes about their work. They are sensitive to being treated as mere "vendors" or "data sources." They want to be treated as partners and professionals.

### What Motivates Them to Say Yes

- "This helps the families I serve."
- "This makes me look good in the community."
- "This costs me nothing — no time, no money, no risk."
- "These people understand what I do and respect it."
- "I have control. I can change things if I want to."

## 3. The Scraping Conversation — How to Handle It

This is the most sensitive part of the entire outreach. Here is exactly how to think about it and what to say.

### The Landscape

Canadian funeral directors are currently angry about obituary scraping. The CBC ran a major story in 2024 headlined "'A moral issue': Canadian funeral directors warn of unauthorized obituaries." The Bereavement Authority of Ontario has received formal complaints. The FSAC is lobbying the federal government for stronger protections.

The bad actors — Echovita, the defunct Afterlife Network — scrape obituaries, rewrite them with AI (sometimes introducing errors), surround them with ads, and sell flowers and digital candles. Families find altered versions of their loved one's obituary on random websites. Funeral directors are furious about this.

**Neshama is not one of these sites.** But the funeral director may not know that yet. The first few seconds of the scraping conversation will determine whether they see Neshama as an ally or a threat.

### How Other Legitimate Platforms Handle This

- **Legacy.com** approached funeral homes by framing syndication as an SEO benefit. Their pitch: "We extend your reach." However, Legacy.com has been widely criticized because their SEO actually outranks the funeral home's own site — out of every 100 people searching for an obituary, roughly 35 click through to Legacy.com and fewer than 2 end up on the funeral home's site. Neshama must avoid this dynamic.

- **Everdays** and similar platforms partner directly with funeral homes, offering pre-planning tools that complement (not replace) funeral home services. The key framing: "We are not a disruptor. We are a complement."

- **Tribute Technology** built a partnership ecosystem where funeral homes opt in and receive integrated services. The emphasis is always on the funeral home remaining in control.

### Neshama's Advantages in This Conversation

Neshama is fundamentally different from the bad actors, and here is why:

1. **We link back to THEIR page.** Every obituary on Neshama links directly to the funeral home's original listing. We drive traffic TO them. This is the opposite of what Echovita does.
2. **We don't modify content.** What families see on Neshama is what the funeral home published. No AI rewriting. No errors. No fabricated details.
3. **We don't sell anything on obituary pages.** No flower upsells, no digital candles, no memorial products alongside their listings. No ads. Ever.
4. **We are not monetizing the obituaries themselves.** Neshama's revenue comes from vendor listings (caterers and food partners), not from the obituary content.
5. **We are offering them control.** They can request changes to how their listings appear, provide a direct data feed, or ask us to adjust anything.

### If They Ask: "Are You Scraping Our Website?"

**Do not dodge this. Do not apologize. Do not get defensive.**

Here is the script:

> "Yes — we pull the obituary listings from your public website and display them alongside the other local funeral homes, so families can search everything in one place. Every listing links directly back to your page. We don't change anything, we don't add ads, and we don't sell anything alongside the obituaries. It's purely a community service. But I completely understand the sensitivity — you may have seen what sites like Echovita have been doing, and that's exactly the kind of thing we built Neshama to be the opposite of. If you'd prefer to provide us with a direct data feed, or if you want to adjust anything about how your listings appear, we are 100% open to that."

### Key Phrases to Use

- "We link back to your page" (say this early and often)
- "We don't change anything"
- "No ads, no products, no upsells on obituary pages"
- "You have full control over how your listings appear"
- "We'd love to work with you directly instead of pulling from the public site"
- "We built this to be the opposite of those scraper sites"

### Key Phrases to AVOID

- "We scrape your site" (use "pull from your public website" or "aggregate your public listings")
- "Your data" (it is not data to them — it is a family's last tribute to someone they loved)
- "Automated" or "bot" (even though it is automated, do not emphasize this)
- "We already have your obituaries" (sounds possessive — say "we feature your listings")
- "Content" (obituaries are not content to them)

## 4. Jordana's Phone Script

### Before the Call

- Have neshama.ca open on your phone or computer
- Have the specific funeral home's listings pulled up so you can reference them
- Have a pen ready — write down the name of whoever answers
- Take a breath. You are not selling. You are introducing yourself.

### The Call

**[Phone rings. Someone answers.]**

> "Hi, good morning. My name is Jordana Mednick. I'm calling about a community project — I'm not a vendor or a salesperson. Could I speak with [the director / the manager / whoever handles community partnerships]?"

**[If they ask what it's about:]**

> "Of course. My friend Erin Kofman and I built a free community resource called Neshama — neshama.ca — that brings together obituaries from local Jewish funeral homes in one place, along with shiva meal coordination tools. We sent an email a few days ago and I just wanted to make sure it landed and introduce myself."

**[If transferred or speaking to the right person:]**

> "Hi [Name], thanks for taking my call. My name is Jordana — I run a catering company called Jem Salads, and through catering shivas every week, I kept seeing the same problem. Families checking three different funeral home websites. Meals overlapping one day and nothing the next. Everyone wants to help but nobody knows where to start.

> So my friend Erin and I built Neshama — it's a free site that pulls together obituaries from the Jewish funeral homes in Toronto and Montreal, and adds meal coordination so the community can organize around a family without the chaos.

> I'm calling because [Funeral Home Name]'s listings are featured on the site. Every one links directly back to your page — we're not trying to take traffic away from you, we're actually sending people to you. I just wanted you to know we exist, and to ask if there's anything about how your listings appear that you'd want us to adjust.

> We sent an email earlier this week — did it come through?"

**[Let them respond. Listen. Do not rush to fill silence.]**

### If They're Receptive

> "That's great to hear. We'd love for this to be a partnership. If you ever want to share Neshama with families during arrangements — maybe as a resource for meal coordination — we'd be honored. And if you'd prefer to send us listings directly instead of us pulling from the website, we can absolutely set that up."

> "Could I send you a quick link so you can see how your listings look on the site?"

### If They're Neutral or Noncommittal

> "I totally understand — you're busy and this came out of nowhere. Would it be okay if I sent you a quick email with a link so you can take a look when you have a moment? No pressure at all."

### If They're Concerned About the Scraping

> [See Section 3 above — use the scraping script verbatim]

### If They Say "We're Not Interested"

> "I completely respect that. The listings will stay linked to your page either way — we're a community resource, not a competitor. But if anything ever changes, or if you'd like us to adjust how your listings appear, my number is [number] and you can always reach us at contact@neshama.ca. Thanks for your time."

### Closing (Any Scenario)

> "Thanks so much for talking with me, [Name]. I really appreciate what you do for families in our community. Have a good day."

## 5. What NOT to Say

These are things that will immediately trigger suspicion, defensiveness, or dismissal from a funeral director.

| Do NOT Say | Why It's Wrong | Say Instead |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| "We're a tech company" | They distrust tech companies disrupting their space | "My friend and I built a community resource" |
| "We scrape your obituaries" | Sounds like Echovita/Afterlife — triggers alarm | "We feature your public listings and link back to your page" |
| "Your content" or "your data" | Obituaries are sacred family documents, not content | "The obituaries" or "the listings families post through you" |
| "We want to partner to grow our platform" | Makes it about you, not them | "We want to make sure this is useful to the families you serve" |
| "We're monetizing..." anything | Raises every red flag in the industry | Only discuss if directly asked — then explain vendor listings are separate from obituaries |
| "We can help you modernize" | Condescending — implies they're behind | "We built something that complements what you already do" |
| "Our algorithm" / "Our bot" / "automated scraping" | Technical language alienates and alarms | "We pull the listings from your public site" |
| "We already have all your obituaries" | Sounds possessive and creepy | "Your listings are featured on the site" |
| "Other funeral homes are on board" | Do not name-drop unless you have explicit permission | "We feature listings from several local funeral homes" |
| "Let me tell you about our features" | Nobody asked for a product demo on the phone | Let them ask questions — answer what they want to know |
| "This is great exposure for you" | Patronizing — they don't need exposure from a startup | "We link back to your page so families can find you" |

## 6. How to Handle Objections

### "We didn't give you permission to use our obituaries."

> "You're right — and that's exactly why I'm calling. I wanted you to know about this and to give you a voice in how it works. The listings are pulled from your public website, and every one links directly back to your page. We don't change anything, we don't add ads, and we don't make money from the obituaries themselves. But if you'd like us to adjust anything — or if you'd prefer to provide a direct feed — we're completely open to that. We want this to work for you."

**Key:** Do not apologize. Do not act like you did something wrong. Be transparent and offer control.

### "We've had problems with sites scraping our obituaries before."

> "I've heard about that — sites like Echovita that take obituaries, rewrite them with AI, add errors, and surround them with ads. That's exactly the kind of thing we built Neshama to be the opposite of. We don't change a word. No ads. No products sold alongside the obituaries. And every listing links back to your page. We're a community resource run by two women in Toronto, not a tech company trying to monetize grief."

### "We need to check with our legal team / management."

> "Of course — take whatever time you need. I'll send you a follow-up email with a link to the site so you can share it internally. And if there are any questions, I'm always available at [number] or contact@neshama.ca."

**Key:** This is actually a good sign. They're taking it seriously. Do NOT push.

### "Can you remove our listings?"

> "Absolutely. If that's what you'd prefer, we'll take them down. But before we do, would you be open to taking a quick look at the site? I think once you see how the listings are presented — with direct links back to your page, no ads, no modifications — you might feel differently. Either way, your call."

**Key:** Always be willing to remove. This is what separates Neshama from the bad actors. But gently suggest they look first.

### "We already work with Legacy.com / Tribute Technology."

> "That's great — those are industry tools. Neshama is something different. It's not a funeral home technology platform. It's a community resource for Jewish families — it brings together obituaries from all the local funeral homes in one search, and adds meal coordination for shiva. Think of it more like a community bulletin board than a tech product. It works alongside what you already have."

### "How do you make money?"

> "Great question. Neshama has a directory of local food and gift vendors for people who want to send something to a shiva family. Some of those vendors pay for featured placement. That's the business side. The obituaries and family-facing tools are completely free, and they always will be. We don't sell anything on obituary pages — no flowers, no candles, no memorial products."

### "I don't have time for this."

> "I completely understand — I won't take any more of your time. Would it be okay if I sent you a one-paragraph email with a link? You can look at it whenever it's convenient, or not at all. Either way, your listings are on the site and they link back to your page. Thanks for what you do for families."

## 7. What a Successful Partnership Looks Like

Not every funeral home will become a partner. Here is the spectrum of outcomes, from minimum to maximum, and all of them are wins.

### Level 1: Awareness (Minimum Win)
- They know Neshama exists
- They've seen their listings on the site
- They haven't asked us to remove anything
- **Why this matters:** They're not going to be blindsided when a family mentions Neshama. They won't feel ambushed.

### Level 2: Passive Acceptance
- They've reviewed the site and are comfortable with how their listings appear
- They may have provided minor feedback (logo placement, formatting)
- No formal partnership, but no objection
- **Why this matters:** Removes the risk of a cease-and-desist or negative word-of-mouth.

### Level 3: Informal Endorsement
- They mention Neshama to families during arrangements ("There's a site called Neshama that can help you coordinate meals")
- They may share neshama.ca with their staff
- **Why this matters:** This is organic, trusted referral from the most authoritative source — the funeral director speaking to a grieving family.

### Level 4: Active Partnership
- They link to Neshama from their website
- They provide a direct data feed instead of us scraping
- They include Neshama in printed materials or resource packets given to families
- They may display a Neshama resource card in their office
- **Why this matters:** This is the full partnership. It validates Neshama in the eyes of the community and creates a sustainable, respectful data relationship.

### Level 5: Co-Creation
- They provide input on features ("families keep asking us about X — can Neshama add that?")
- They refer families directly to Neshama's meal coordination tool
- They participate in community events or content with us
- **Why this matters:** This turns a data source into a true partner. This is the long game.

### What to Aim For on the First Call

**Level 1 or 2.** That is it. The goal of the first call is:

1. They know who you are
2. They know the site exists
3. They've been offered control
4. The door is open for future conversation

Do not try to close a partnership on the first call. These relationships develop over months and years, not minutes.

## 9. The Emotional Landscape — What Jordana Should Know

Funeral directors are exhausted. They deal with death every day. They absorb the grief of families who are shattered. They are underappreciated by society, stereotyped in media, and approached by dozens of vendors trying to sell them things every week.

When Jordana calls, she is entering their world. The single most important thing she can do is **demonstrate that she understands what they do and respects it.**

The line "Thanks for what you do for families" is not a throwaway. It should be said with sincerity. Most people never say this to funeral directors.

Jordana's personal story — her mother reading obituaries, losing her to MSA, carrying on the tradition through catering shivas — is the most powerful tool in this entire playbook. It is not a pitch. It is the truth. And funeral directors, who spend their lives serving people through loss, will recognize it immediately.

**Use the story. But only when the moment is right — when they ask why you built this, or when you feel genuine connection on the call. Never deploy it mechanically.**

## 10. After the Call — Follow-Up Protocol

### If the call went well:
1. Send a brief thank-you email within 2 hours
2. Include a direct link to their listings on Neshama
3. Reiterate: "If anything about how your listings appear needs adjusting, just let me know"
4. Do NOT ask for anything in this email — no partnership ask, no link request
5. Wait 2-3 weeks before any further outreach

### If the call was neutral:
1. Send the follow-up email with the link
2. Keep it to 3-4 sentences max
3. Wait 3-4 weeks before any further outreach
4. Look for organic touchpoints (community events, mutual connections)

### If the call was negative:
1. Send a brief, gracious email: "Thank you for your time. If anything changes, we're always here."
2. Do NOT push back or try to change their mind
3. Wait 2-3 months before any further outreach
4. If they asked for removal, remove their listings promptly and confirm by email

### If you left a voicemail and they haven't called back:
1. Wait 5 business days
2. Send a short email: "Hi [Name], I left a voicemail earlier this week about Neshama. Here's a quick link so you can see how [Funeral Home Name]'s listings appear: [link]. Happy to chat whenever it's convenient."
3. After that, wait. Do not call again for at least 3 weeks.
